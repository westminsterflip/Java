History[edit]

Egyptian hieroglyph "n"-Phoenician
Mem		Etruscan MGreek
Mu		Roman M

The letter M is derived from the Phoenician Mem, via the Greek Mu (╬ю, ╬╝). Semitic Mem probably originally pictured water. It is thought that Semitic people working in Egypt c. 2000 BC borrowed a hieroglyph for "water" that was first used for an alveolar nasal (/n/), because of the Egyptian word for water, n-t. This same symbol became used for /m/ in Semitic, because the word for water began with that sound.

Use in writing systems[edit]

The letter РЪеmРЪЕ represents the bilabial nasal consonant sound [m] in the orthography of Latin as well as in that of many modern languages, and also in the International Phonetic Alphabet. In English, the Oxford English Dictionary (first edition) says that РЪеmРЪЕ is sometimes a vowel in words like spasm and in the suffix -ism. In modern terminology, this is described as a syllabic consonant (IPA [m╠Е]).

Other uses[edit]

The Roman numeral РЁ» represents the number 1000, though it was not used in Roman times.[2]
