Eá¸·u, also Hela or Helu, is a Middle Indo-Iranian language or Prakrit of the 3rd century BCE. It is ancestral to the Sinhalese language. R. C. Childers, in the Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society, states:

[Elu] is the name by which is known an ancient form of the Sinhala language from which the modern vernacular of Ceylon is immediately received, and to which the latter bears is of the same relation that the English of today bears to Anglo-Saxon...The name Elu is no other than Sinhala much succeeded, standing for an older form, HÄ•la or HÄ•lu, which occurs in some ancient works, and this again for a still older, SÄ•la, which brings us back to the Pali SÃ®hala.[1]

The Pali scholar Thomas William Rhys Davids refers to Eá¸·u as "the Prakrit of Ceylon".[2]

The Hela Havula are a modern Sri Lankan literary organization that advocate the use of Eá¸·u terms over Sanskritisms. Eá¸·u is often referred to by modern Sinhalese as amisra, Sinhalese for "unmixed".

A feature of Eá¸·u is its preference for short vowels, loss of aspiration and the reduction of compound consonants found frequently in other Prakrits such as Pali.

Eá¸·u in comparison with Pali and Sanskrit[edit]

Being a Prakrit, Eá¸·u is closely related to other Prakrits such as Pali. Indeed, a very large proportion of Eá¸·u word-stems are identical in form to Pali. The connections were sufficiently well known that technical terms from Pali and Sanskrit were easily converted into Eá¸·u by a set of conventional phonological transformations. Because of the prevalence of these transformations, it is not always possible to tell whether a given Eá¸·u word is a part of the old Prakrit lexicon, or a transformed borrowing from Sanskrit.
