History[edit]

The Semitic sound value of Q√¥p (perhaps originally qaw, "cord of wool", and possibly based on an Egyptian hieroglyph) was /q/ (voiceless uvular stop), a sound common to Semitic languages, but not found in English or most[citation needed] Indo-European ones. In Greek, this sign as Qoppa œò probably came to represent several labialized velar stops, among them /k ∑/ and /k ∑ ∞/. As a result of later sound shifts, these sounds in Greek changed to /p/ and /p ∞/ respectively. Therefore, Qoppa was transformed into two letters: Qoppa, which stood for a number only, and Phi Œ¶ which stood for the aspirated sound /p ∞/ that came to be pronounced /f/ in Modern Greek.[citation needed]

In the earliest Latin inscriptions, the letters C, K and Q were all used to represent the two sounds /k/ and /…°/, which were not differentiated in writing. Of these, Q was used before a rounded vowel (e.g. ‚ü®EQO‚ü© 'ego'), K before /a/, and C elsewhere. Later, the use of C (and its variant G) replaced most usages of K and Q: Q survived only to represent /k/ when immediately followed by a /w/ sound.[2] The Etruscans used Q in conjunction with V to represent /k ∑/.

Use in writing systems[edit]

English[edit]

In English, the digraph ‚ü®qu‚ü© most often denotes the cluster /kw/; however, in borrowings from French, it represents /k/, as in 'plaque'. See the list of English words containing Q not followed by U. ‚ü®q‚ü© is the second-least-common letter in the English language, with a frequency of just 0.09% in words. Only ‚ü®z‚ü© occurs less often.

Other languages[edit]

In most European languages written in the Latin script, such as in Romance and Germanic languages, ‚ü®q‚ü© appears almost exclusively in the digraph ‚ü®qu‚ü©. Notable exceptions to this are Albanian, in which ‚ü®q‚ü© represents the voiceless palatal stop [c], and Maltese and V√µro, which use it to represent the glottal stop [ î]. In French, Occitan, Catalan and Portuguese, ‚ü®qu‚ü© represents /k/ or /kw/; in Spanish, it represents /k/. ‚ü®qu‚ü© replaces ‚ü®c‚ü© for /k/ before front vowels ‚ü®i‚ü© and ‚ü®e‚ü©, since in those languages ‚ü®c‚ü© represents a fricative or affricate before front vowels. In Italian ‚ü®qu‚ü© represents [kw] (where [w] is the semivowel allophone of /u/).

It is not considered to be part of the Bosnian, Croatian, Estonian, Icelandic, Irish, Latvian, Lithuanian, Polish, Scottish Gaelic, Slovenian, Turkish, or Welsh alphabet.

‚ü®q‚ü© has a wide variety of pronunciations among non-European languages that have adopted the Latin alphabet. It has the value /q/ in Aymara, Crimean Tatar, Greenlandic, Quechua, Uyghur and Uzbek. In Azerbaijani, ‚ü®q‚ü© stands for a voiced velar stop […°]. In Chinese Hanyu Pinyin, ‚ü®q‚ü© is used to represent the sound [t…ï ∞], which is close to English ‚ü®ch‚ü© in "cheese", but pronounced further toward the front of the mouth. ‚ü®q‚ü© in Fijian has the value of a prenasalized voiced velar stop [≈ã…°]. In Kiowa, ‚ü®q‚ü© represents a glottalized velar stop [k º]. In Xhosa and Zulu, ‚ü®q‚ü© is used for the postalveolar click [k«É]. In transliteration of Classical Mongolian, ‚ü®q‚ü© represents a voiceless velar fricative [x].

Other systems[edit]

The International Phonetic Alphabet uses ‚ü®q‚ü© for the voiceless uvular stop.

Other uses[edit]

The Roman numeral Q is sometimes used to represent the number 500,000.[3]

Forms and variants[edit]

A comparison of the glyphs of ‚ü®q‚ü© and ‚ü®g‚ü©.

The lowercase Q (q) is usually seen as a lowercase O with a descender (i.e., downward vertical tail) extending from the right side of the bowl, with or without a swash (i.e., flourish), even a reversed lowercase p. The lowercase Q's descender is usually typed without a swash due to the major style difference typically seen between the descenders of the lowercase G (a loop) and lowercase Q (vertical). The descender of the lowercase Q is sometimes handwritten finishing with a rightward swash to distinguish from the leftward facing curved descender on the lowercase G or the number 9.
